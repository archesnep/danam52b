Heritage Walks – come along!

NHDP is not only documenting individual sites of endangered heritage in the Kathmandu Valley or in West Nepal. The team also records the use made of the sites and how they contribute to ritual practice – which is central in keeping the heritage alive. Often, sites are related to each other, for instance, through ritual practice such as processions. In fact, in places like the royal city of Patan we can see that processions still play an important role for communities. We have selected a few procession walks for you that will be shared with you over the next months.

The NHDP team invites you to join us in exploring the fascinating patterns of temples, monasteries and shrines, wells and fountains) or arcaded platforms and rest houses .

We will regularly inform about the heritage walks, and hope that you will walk along!

The maps were designed by Elias Michaels, who georeferenced the historic basemaps and aerial pictures and integrated them in the interactive framework, allowing a multimedia perspective on the heritage walks and its locations.

Following the Buddha: About the festival for the Buddha of Light

The Dīpaṅkara Buddha, or ‘Maker of Light’, is said to predate the historical Gautama Buddha, and is often equated with the first Buddha (Michaels 2013: 2). Pañcadāna (lit. ‘five offerings’) is a festival in which the initiated Śākya and Vajrācārya males have to manifest their monastic status by going from house to house to collect alms from at least seven houses. In the historic city of Patan, this festival, is known as Yala (Patan) Pañcadāna. It is celebrated on the 8th lunar day of the bright fortnight of the Śrāvaṇa month according to the lunar calendar; in 2020, that day fell on July 28.

On the day of Pañcadāna, initiated male lay devotees offer alms to the Vajracārya priests and Sākya householder monks. Of central importance is Phudyaḥ - a c. 60 cm high gold-plated copper icon of Dīpaṅkara Buddha. The icon of the deity is carried from house to house, from monastery to monastery, by the Śākyas of Ukubahāl monastery. When the deity visits the selected places, it is worshipped with gifts (dāna). Offerings are mainly rice grains, wheat grains, salt, fruits, money and the likes.

About the procession route

The Phudyaḥ jātrā continues from the morning until late evening. The icon of Phudyaḥ, usually kept in the Ukubaha monastery, is cleaned one day before and on the next day, in the morning on the very day of the jātrā, it is displayed in the northern wing of the monastery with its ornaments and full decorations. At that time, local residents, mainly Śākyas of the locality, come to offer their pūjā and alms to the deity. After that the jātrā begins its traditional route (see the route map).

The route can be divided into several parts. The first circumambulation in the morning starts from Ukubaha and follows the localities accordingly: (1) Sundhara, (2) Guita, (3) Subaha, (4) Bhinchebaha, (5) Tyāga, (6) Tvaybaha, (7) Yacchubaha, (8) Yanamugal, (9) Gujibaha, (10) Jyobabahi, (11) Naudobaha, (12) Jyothabaha, (13) Sikuncabaha, (14) Hitiphusabaha, (15) Nagubaha, (16) Kanibaha, (17) Ubabahi, (18) Dunebaha, (19) Mahabauddha, (20) Ukubaha. Even upon returning to its home-monastery, the icon of the deity does not enter the vihara. It remains outside while human participants are allowed to enter the monastery to have their lunch). The second circumambulation – from noon to evening – starts form Ukubaha and follows these localities: (1) Gangabaha, (2) Dhandyabaha, (3) Dyanani (4) Bakunani, (5) Darbar, (6) Tangabaha, (7) Cukabaha, (8) Tabaha, (9) Jombaha, (10) Nhaykanbahi, (11) Talache, (12) Kvache, (13) Bahalukha, (14) Kaygunani, (15) Daubaha, (16) Vanabaha, (17) Habaha, (18) Nabaha, (19) Kalanche, (20) Mikhabaha, (21) Nyakhacuka, (22) Nakabahi, (23) Khache, (24) Nagbaha, (25) Sasunani, (26) Kontibahi, (27) Mesahiti and comes back to Ukubaha.

The affect of the COVID-19 Pandemic on the festival:

As almost all festivals of the Kathmandu Valley were affected by COVID-19 pandemic, either banned, delayed or very simply celebrated, this Phudyaḥ jātrā was also nearly put on halt in 2020. On July 20, a big discussion about organizing the Pañcadāna festival was held among the executive members of Organization of Buddhist Monasteries of Patan, representatives of all Buddhist monasteries of Patan, of the Committee for the study on Vajrācārya Pūjā and Rituals (Vajrācārya Pūjā Vidhi Addhyayan Samiti) and of the Organization of Traditional Buddhadharma of Nepal (Nepala Paramparāgata Bauddhadharma Saṃgha). They unanimously decided to celebrate the Pañcadāna festival only under the limited boundary of their own monastery to prevent the spreading of COVID-19. According to a senior member of the vihāra, the members of Ukubahā monastery decided to continue the Phudyaḥ Jātrā because several devotees would not take their food without offering ritual gifts (dāna) to Phudyaḥ on that day. They also decided that without invitation, the jātrā members would not enter any vihāra or house.
